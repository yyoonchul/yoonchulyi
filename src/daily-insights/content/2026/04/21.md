### 3. Are the Costs of AI Agents Also Rising Exponentially?

1. Ord's core argument is that capability trends need to be paired with cost trends.
   - He says METR's well-known charts show the length of tasks AI agents can complete growing exponentially over roughly seven years.
   - His concern is that people extrapolate those time horizons without asking what it costs to achieve them.
   - He defines the key metric as the hourly cost of using an LLM at the model's 50% time horizon.
2. The essay argues that rising capability may be partly purchased with rising compute spend.
   - Ord writes that model parameter counts increased by about 4,000x over the period in question.
   - He also says the number of tokens generated per task rose by roughly 100,000x.
   - That makes it plausible, in his view, that peak benchmark performance is being pushed upward by increasingly expensive inference budgets.
3. He uses METR cost-performance curves to derive "sweet spots" and "saturation points."
   - The sweet spot is the lowest hourly cost a model achieves before diminishing returns set in.
   - The saturation point is a later stage where time-horizon gains become very expensive relative to additional compute.
   - Ord argues those annotations reveal more about practical economics than raw plateau performance alone.
4. The concrete examples suggest wide variation and some cases close to human labor costs.
   - He says a human software engineer is at best around $120 per hour on his chart, while model sweet spots range from about $40 per hour for o3 to around $0.40 per hour for Grok 4 and Sonnet 3.5.
   - The same essay says costs can rise sharply near plateaus, with GPT-5 at about $13 per hour for roughly 45-minute tasks but about $120 per hour for 2-hour tasks.
   - Ord also says o3 can reach about $350 per hour when pushed to its full 1.5-hour time horizon.
5. His conclusion is suggestive rather than final.
   - The article says there is moderate evidence that both total costs and even hourly costs are rising with longer frontier task horizons.
   - Ord warns that this could create a growing gap between what is possible in principle and what is economically deployable in practice.
   - He also lists clear limitations, including chart glitches, estimated OpenAI price points, the need for more models, and the need to plot hourly cost directly against release date.
