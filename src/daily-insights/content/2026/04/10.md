## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. Is Anthropic limiting the release of Mythos to protect the internet — or Anthropic?

1. Anthropic is presenting Mythos as a model too capable to distribute broadly.
   - TechCrunch says Anthropic limited release of Mythos because it can find security exploits in widely used software more effectively than prior models.
   - Instead of a public launch, the company is sharing the model with large organizations that operate critical online infrastructure.
   - The named examples include major institutions such as Amazon Web Services and JPMorgan Chase.
2. The article treats the cybersecurity rationale as plausible but incomplete.
   - OpenAI is reportedly considering a similar controlled rollout for its own cybersecurity tooling, which suggests this is becoming a wider frontier-lab pattern.
   - At the same time, TechCrunch flags the ambiguity in the word "exploitability" because a discovered weakness is not automatically a high-value attack path.
   - AI security lab Irregular's CEO Dan Lahav told TechCrunch that what matters is whether the model found something meaningfully exploitable on its own or as part of a chain.
3. Anthropic's absolute technical lead is presented as contestable rather than settled.
   - The company says Mythos is much stronger than Opus at exploiting vulnerabilities.
   - But startup Aisle says it replicated much of Anthropic's claimed performance with smaller open-weight models.
   - That counterpoint supports the idea that cybersecurity performance may depend on workflows and task design, not just one dominant frontier model.
4. Selective release also fits an enterprise go-to-market strategy.
   - Limiting access to big companies creates a premium capability that can strengthen large commercial contracts.
   - It also keeps the most capable model tier away from smaller labs and independent builders.
   - TechCrunch cites startup CEO David Crawshaw's argument that this kind of gating can function as enterprise packaging as much as public-safety policy.
5. Distillation pressure gives frontier labs another reason to gate top-tier models.
   - TechCrunch says enterprise-only access makes it harder for competitors to use model outputs for distillation.
   - The article points to broader industry defensiveness, including Anthropic's public claims about copy attempts and a Bloomberg report that Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI are coordinating against model copying.
   - Its conclusion is cautious rather than accusatory: Anthropic may be protecting internet security, but the same move also protects margins and competitive position.

### 2. Memory for humans and agents

1. The post starts by reframing the core memory problem as retrieval rather than capture.
   - Jeong says he has already tried multiple systems, including PARA, Zettelkasten, Johnny Decimal, Obsidian daily notes, Notion databases, and unstructured Apple Notes.
   - The recurring failure is not that information never gets written down, but that earlier decisions become hard to recover later.
   - He uses a concrete example of failing to find a February product decision after searching through meetings, Slack, and notes.
2. He separates memory into two layers with different operating models.
   - Short-term memory is described as a timeline of what happened today, which makes daily notes the natural format.
   - Long-term memory is described as a wiki that stores accumulated decisions, beliefs, people, and lessons in a form meant for traversal.
   - The design claim is that these two layers should not be forced into the same organizational shape.
3. Existing productivity systems fail because they ask humans to be librarians.
   - He says frameworks like PARA and Zettelkasten are directionally right about the need for structure.
   - Their practical weakness is that they rely on constant filing, categorization, and link maintenance by the user.
   - His argument is that entropy wins because humans are structurally bad at sustaining thousands of low-reward organization decisions.
4. The proposed fix is an agent-maintained knowledge base built from messy raw inputs.
   - Jeong points to Andrej Karpathy's `pedia` example, which reportedly generated 400 articles from 2,500 diary entries, Apple Notes, iMessage conversations, and startup records.
   - He prefers that explicit, inspectable artifact to a black-box assistant that merely claims to "know" the user better over time.
   - The model is that humans keep capturing naturally while an AI librarian extracts decisions, updates the wiki, and links new facts to old context.
5. The idea expands from personal memory into team memory and coordination.
   - If each person has a daily note and a maintained wiki, a team can also gain a shared memory layer that updates itself.
   - In that setup, decisions from one person's meeting can become context in another person's workflow instead of staying trapped in isolated chat history.
   - Jeong says that is where the agent becomes more than a librarian and starts acting as a triage layer that routes tasks, surfaces conflicts, and recalls relevant context before anyone asks.
