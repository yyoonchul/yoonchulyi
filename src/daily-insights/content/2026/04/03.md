## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. Tesla's cheaper vehicles aren't helping its declining sales

1. Tesla finally introduced the low-cost trims it had been promising, but the launch did not reverse the demand problem.
   - The company started selling a rear-wheel-drive Model Y priced at $39,990 and a smaller-battery rear-wheel-drive Model 3 at $36,990.
   - Those trims were meant to defend volume without waiting for an entirely new platform.
   - Even with the cheaper variants in market, quarterly deliveries still came in below consensus expectations.
2. The headline numbers show a quarter that was weak both relative to forecasts and relative to Tesla's historical growth narrative.
   - Tesla reported 358,023 deliveries in the first quarter of 2026.
   - Analysts had expected roughly 368,000 deliveries, so the company still missed despite aggressive pricing action.
   - TechCrunch frames the result as more evidence that Tesla's old growth formula is no longer working cleanly.
3. Production continues to run ahead of customer demand, which keeps pressure on inventory and margins.
   - Tesla produced 408,386 vehicles in the quarter, far above the number it delivered.
   - That gap suggests Tesla is still building more cars than it can move at its preferred price points.
   - When discounting or low-cost trims become the main sales tool, the risk is that unit growth comes at the expense of profitability.
4. The year-over-year comparison is not especially reassuring because last year's base was already distorted and weak.
   - Deliveries were only about 6% above the same quarter in 2025.
   - TechCrunch notes that Q1 2025 was already one of Tesla's weakest quarters in years because production lines had been shut down to prepare the refreshed Model Y.
   - Beating that low bar by a modest amount does not change the broader concern that Tesla could be headed for another down year.
5. The product roadmap remains the core strategic issue behind the sales weakness.
   - Tesla's only truly new recent model is the Cybertruck, and the article says it has not met the company's earlier expectations.
   - Tesla previously scrapped the planned roughly $25,000 EV in favor of its autonomous CyberCab bet, so it still lacks a fresh mainstream model.
   - The article treats the cheaper Model 3 and Model Y trims as a stopgap, not a convincing answer to the company's longer-term growth problem.

---

### 2. OpenAI's shocking fall from grace as investors race to Anthropic

1. The clearest signal in the article is that secondary-market investor demand has shifted sharply away from OpenAI and toward Anthropic.
   - About a half-dozen institutional holders recently tried to sell roughly $600 million of OpenAI exposure through one marketplace.
   - The platform operator says that in earlier periods those shares would likely have cleared within days, but this time no buyers appeared.
   - On the other side of the market, investors on the same platform were ready with around $2 billion in cash for Anthropic shares.
2. The pricing gap between the two companies is now being interpreted as a catch-up story for Anthropic rather than a clear lead for OpenAI.
   - The article says Anthropic is attracting bids that imply valuations near $600 billion on some secondary venues.
   - That is described as more than 50% above Anthropic's prior round, showing how quickly investor enthusiasm has moved.
   - By contrast, OpenAI bids are cited around $765 billion, which is about a 10% discount to its previous roughly $850 billion valuation.
3. Market structure matters here because private-company share trading is restricted and often routed through intermediaries.
   - These transactions are not fully endorsed company tenders and can involve SPVs or other wrappers to get exposure.
   - Banks including Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs are described as offering private-market access to wealthy clients.
   - The article uses those channels to argue that current investor preference is visible even if exact clearing prices are still opaque.
4. The core reason investors give is that Anthropic now looks better positioned in enterprise AI than OpenAI.
   - The article says OpenAI still dominates in consumer mindshare, but it spends heavily on infrastructure and has moved more slowly in profitable enterprise accounts.
   - Anthropic is described as stronger with higher-margin enterprise customers, which makes its revenue profile look more attractive to investors.
   - That combination makes Anthropic feel like the cleaner near-term upside trade even though OpenAI remains the bigger brand.
5. The sentiment shift is notable, but the article also makes clear that neither company is free of risk or strategic complexity.
   - OpenAI had just announced a $122 billion fundraising round, the largest ever for a startup, so it is hardly capital-constrained.
   - Anthropic is dealing with a Pentagon supply-chain-risk lawsuit and with fallout from accidentally releasing internal source code.
   - Even with those caveats, the article says both firms are weighing IPO paths and that investors currently see Anthropic as the more attractive private AI bet.
