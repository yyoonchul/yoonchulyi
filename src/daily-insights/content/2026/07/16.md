## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. How to read more books

1. Scotto treats reading volume as a habit-design problem, not a calendar problem.
   - Scotto says he now reads roughly a book a week after starting from fewer than ten books per year.
   - The central advice is to read whenever nothing else is happening, instead of trying to reserve special reading time.
   - The main competition for reading is the default impulse to pick up a phone during idle moments.
   - Serious readers, in Scotto's framing, replace that reflex with reaching for a book.
2. The phone is the biggest obstacle in the system.
   - Scotto removed social media and streaming apps such as Instagram, YouTube, and Facebook from his iPhone.
   - After the removal, the phone became less rewarding because it mainly offered weather, email, or banking checks.
   - He describes a short adjustment period in which his brain stopped expecting instant phone stimulation.
   - A cheap analog watch also helps because checking the time no longer requires opening the phone.
3. Books need to be physically available in small gaps.
   - Scotto recommends always carrying a book, even when it may not be used.
   - He reads after waking up, before sleep, while cooking or eating, on public transport, and while waiting during errands.
   - Public transport is especially useful because someone else drives while the reader gets uninterrupted time.
   - Ebook readers solve the portability problem by carrying many books, offering backlights, highlights, and definitions.
4. Reading choice should stay flexible.
   - Scotto reads multiple books in parallel so that one boring option does not block the habit.
   - He recommends mixing fiction and nonfiction and reading broadly across genres and subjects.
   - The guiding phrase is to "read what you like until you like to read."
   - He also encourages quitting books without treating an unfinished book as a personal failure.
5. Goals, reviews, and discovery tools help, but shortcuts undermine the point.
   - Goodreads Reading Challenge-style goals can motivate consistency, but counting books can also distort reading quality.
   - Writing reviews and revisiting highlights helps books stay in memory and improves understanding.
   - Goodreads and YouTube help with recommendations, comments, and spoiler-free reviews.
   - Scotto advises against speed reading, summaries as replacements, and audiobooks as substitutes because focused reading requires full attention.

### 2. I love LLMs, I hate hype

   - The argument is not anti-AI; it is anti-hype around AI.
2. The first target is negative-valence AI hype.
   - Hotz rejects claims that an opportunity window is closing or that people will fall permanently behind.
   - He characterizes these claims as designed to make people feel bad about themselves.

3. The second target is exaggerated superintelligence storytelling.
   - Hotz criticizes the leap from "better autocomplete, compiler, or search engine" to cosmic takeover narratives.
   - He points to earlier superintelligence talks and machine-takeover fiction to argue that these ideas are not new.
   - The post says some AI groups claim credit for progress that would happen through broader computing trends anyway.
   - This connects to Hotz's recurring argument that frontier labs may create value but fail to capture it.
4. Open source and commodification are central to the economic argument.
   - Hotz argues that anti-open-source positions are partly driven by fear of AI commodification.
   - He frames AI progress as flowing from Moore's law and general computing progress, not only from frontier labs.

5. Hotz updates his view on coding agents while keeping caveats.

### 3. In defense of not understanding your codebase

1. Goedecke distinguishes small-codebase and large-codebase programming cultures.
   - Small, low-turnover teams may reasonably expect engineers to understand the whole system.
   - Large, high-turnover organizations such as Google or GitHub cannot expect complete understanding from any one engineer.
   - Goedecke argues that online software discussion overrepresents the small-codebase perspective.
   - The essay defends partial understanding as a legitimate state in many professional environments.
2. The post challenges Peter Naur's strongest theory-building claim.
   - Naur argued that the real product of programming is a team's "theory of the program," not just the code.
   - Goedecke accepts that code and documentation capture only part of a team's understanding.
   - The disagreement is with Naur's claim that lost theory cannot be reconstructed and that the old program should be discarded.
   - Goedecke says effective engineers at large companies know abandoned systems can be revived.
3. Large systems cannot simply be rewritten from scratch.
   - Big user-facing systems contain thousands of quirks and edge cases that are difficult to rediscover or reimplement.
   - Successful rewrites usually start by carving the existing system into isolated pieces.
   - That means replacement still depends on changing and understanding parts of the old system.
   - Goedecke notes that codebases with no remaining experts are common in companies with millions of lines and thousands of engineers.
4. Partial theories are unavoidable and still useful.
   - In sufficiently large systems, everyone holds an incomplete or partly incorrect theory of the program.
   - Effective engineers start with one end-to-end flow and branch outward through careful changes.
   - Local reasoning is necessary because no perfect expert may exist to answer every question.
   - Confidence, educated guesses, and willingness to handle consequences become part of the job.
5. Theory-building is one engineering value among others.
   - Goedecke says LLMs may weaken detailed theory-building while helping engineers build useful partial theories faster.
   - Other ordinary workplace realities also weaken total understanding, including team turnover, legal requirements, security upgrades, and dependencies.
   - Maintaining a full mental model can be fun and valuable, especially in small open-source projects.
   - At work, engineers are paid to adopt the organization's tradeoffs among speed, compliance, politics, maintainability, and understanding.

### 4. Why write code in 2026

1. Turnbull frames modern engineering as building the software factory.
   - The post says software engineers increasingly maintain the assembly line that lets anyone prompt for a change and ship quickly.
   - That factory includes proactive context such as prompts, skills, AGENTS.md files, and knowledge bases.
   - It also includes reactive protection through automated evaluation, tests, linting, type systems, evals, and other AI.
   - Within those constraints, even a weaker model with fresh context can often produce an acceptable change.
2. The author still rejects the idea that humans should stop writing code.
   - Turnbull says writing code is valuable even with very capable AI agents.
   - The reason is not that agents are worse than humans at coding, but that code lets humans think directly in the execution environment.
   - English is under-specified for precise computation, especially in algorithmic work.
   - Executable steps give a calibrated level of precision that prompting alone may not provide.
3. Hands-on coding creates attention, ownership, and architectural understanding.
   - Turnbull says passive review of diffs and patches does not create the same connection to a system.
   - Writing code exposes fragility: if a human struggles to build on the code without breaking it, an agent may also struggle.
   - Cleaning up a pattern and documenting a consistent principle can make the software factory work better.
   - Debugging can reveal weak testing strategy and remove entire classes of bugs.
4. Agents can preserve and amplify bad decisions.
   - Turnbull compares coding agents less to compilers and more to freshly onboarded interns.
   - Agents often make the current change conservatively rather than improving the surrounding architecture.
   - The post gives an example where an offhand human idea to use browser local storage led to extra wrapping and indirection.
   - Hands-on exploration and deleting code helped the author reach a better architecture than explaining everything through English.
5. The software factory still needs deep inspection of details.
   - Agents have pushed more engineers to add CI, measurement, evaluation, and guardrails early, which Turnbull sees as a major improvement.
   - But assembly lines have weak spots that require occasional disassembly and close observation.
   - Engineers may need to inspect algorithms, performance, or test failures in detail to produce meaningful improvements.
   - The goal is to connect minute implementation details to the big picture, not to draw rigid boundaries around what humans should touch.

### 5. A Framework for Frontier AI and the Dawning of a New Age _(X Article)_

1. Hassabis presents AGI as an imminent and historically unusual technology.
   - Hassabis writes that artificial general intelligence may be only a few years away.
   - The article defines AGI as a system with all the cognitive capabilities of the human brain.
   - Hassabis compares AGI less to the internet or mobile and more to electricity or fire.
   - The article argues that AGI could produce impact at 10 times the Industrial Revolution's scale and 10 times its speed.
2. The upside is paired with a warning about frontier risks.
   - Hassabis says AI could accelerate drug discovery, clean energy, advanced materials, and broader abundance.
   - At the same time, the article points to cybersecurity risks already visible in frontier models.
   - Other threats, including nuclear and biological risks, may emerge as capabilities continue advancing.
   - More agentic and recursively self-improving systems may require safeguards that preserve human control.
3. The core proposal is a Frontier AI Standards Body.
   - Hassabis proposes a US-led standards body modeled on a federally overseen public-private partnership or self-regulatory organization such as FINRA.
   - The body would include independent technical experts and open-source representatives.
   - Funding would likely come mostly from industry to attract strong technical talent and provide compute for large-scale testing.
   - The body would define benchmarks for "Frontier-class" models and identify qualifying "Frontier Labs."
4. The evaluation regime would start voluntary and become more formal.
   - Initially, Frontier Labs would voluntarily share models up to 30 days before release.
   - Once the assessment protocol proved effective, qualifying models could be required to pass it before US market deployment.
   - Tests would cover cybersecurity, biological threats, other high-risk domains, guardrail bypasses, deception, watermarking, and human-readable reasoning outputs.
   - Evaluations would be updated regularly, perhaps quarterly at first, with saturated benchmarks deprecated and independent held-out tests developed over time.
5. The broader aim is international coordination and social choice.
   - Hassabis says the US effort could become a starting point for shared international frontier AI standards.
   - The framework would apply to frontier-class models regardless of origin or open-versus-closed status, while exempting non-frontier models from startups and academia.
   - The article says technical challenges are only part of the problem; economic and philosophical questions about post-scarcity life also matter.
   - Hassabis argues that society should use the window before AGI arrives to shape the technology toward broad human benefit.

### 3. In defense of not understanding your codebase

1. Goedecke는 작은 코드베이스 문화와 큰 코드베이스 문화를 구분한다.
   - 작고 인력 이동이 적은 팀은 엔지니어가 전체 시스템을 이해해야 한다고 기대할 수 있다.
   - 구글(Google)이나 깃허브(GitHub) 같은 크고 인력 이동이 많은 조직은 한 엔지니어에게 완전한 이해를 기대할 수 없다.
   - Goedecke는 온라인 소프트웨어 논의가 작은 코드베이스 관점을 과대표집한다고 주장한다.
   - 이 글은 많은 전문 환경에서 부분적 이해가 정당한 상태라고 옹호한다.
2. 글은 Peter Naur의 가장 강한 이론 구축(theory-building) 주장을 비판한다.
   - Naur는 프로그래밍의 진짜 산물이 코드 자체가 아니라 팀의 "프로그램 이론(theory of the program)"이라고 주장했다.
   - Goedecke는 코드와 문서가 팀의 이해 일부만 포착한다는 점은 받아들인다.
   - 의견 차이는 잃어버린 이론을 재구성할 수 없으므로 기존 프로그램을 폐기해야 한다는 Naur의 주장에 있다.
   - Goedecke는 대기업의 유능한 엔지니어라면 버려진 시스템도 되살릴 수 있음을 안다고 말한다.
3. 대형 시스템은 처음부터 다시 쓸 수 없다.
   - 사용자가 있는 큰 시스템에는 다시 발견하거나 재구현하기 어려운 수천 개의 특이 사례와 예외가 들어 있다.
   - 성공적인 재작성(rewrite)은 보통 기존 시스템을 고립된 작은 조각으로 나누는 데서 시작한다.
   - 이는 대체 작업도 결국 기존 시스템 일부를 바꾸고 이해하는 일에 의존한다는 뜻이다.
   - Goedecke는 수백만 줄과 수천 명의 엔지니어가 있는 회사에서는 남아 있는 전문가가 없는 코드베이스가 흔하다고 말한다.
4. 부분적 이론은 피할 수 없고 그래도 유용하다.
   - 충분히 큰 시스템에서는 모두가 프로그램에 대한 불완전하거나 부분적으로 틀린 이론을 갖는다.
   - 유능한 엔지니어는 하나의 처음부터 끝까지 이어지는 흐름(end-to-end flow)에서 시작해 신중한 변경을 하며 바깥으로 뻗어 간다.
   - 모든 질문에 답해 줄 완벽한 전문가가 없을 수 있으므로 국소적 추론이 필요하다.
   - 자신감, 교육받은 추측, 결과를 감당하려는 태도도 업무의 일부가 된다.
5. 이론 구축은 여러 엔지니어링 가치 중 하나다.
   - Goedecke는 대규모 언어 모델이 세밀한 이론 구축을 약화할 수 있지만, 유용한 부분 이론을 더 빠르게 만들도록 도울 수도 있다고 말한다.
   - 팀 이탈, 법적 요구사항, 보안 업그레이드, 의존성(dependency) 같은 평범한 업무 현실도 전체 이해를 약화한다.
   - 완전한 정신 모델(mental model)을 유지하는 일은 특히 작은 오픈소스 프로젝트에서는 즐겁고 가치 있을 수 있다.
   - 직장에서 엔지니어는 속도, 준수(compliance), 조직 정치, 유지보수성, 이해 사이에서 조직이 택한 절충을 받아들이는 대가로 보수를 받는다.

### 4. Why write code in 2026

1. Turnbull은 현대 엔지니어링을 소프트웨어 공장을 만드는 일로 설명한다.

   - 이런 제약 안에서는 비교적 약한 모델도 새로운 맥락을 받으면 수용 가능한 변경을 만들 수 있다.
2. 작성자는 그래도 사람이 코드를 쓰지 않아도 된다는 생각을 거부한다.
   - Turnbull은 매우 강력한 AI 에이전트가 있어도 코드를 쓰는 일이 가치 있다고 말한다.
   - 이유는 에이전트가 사람보다 코딩을 못해서가 아니라, 코드가 사람이 실행 환경(execution environment) 안에서 직접 생각하게 해 주기 때문이다.
   - 영어는 정확한 계산을 표현하기에는 덜 규정된 언어이며, 알고리즘 작업에서는 특히 그렇다.
   - 실행 가능한 단계는 프롬프트만으로는 얻기 어려운 보정된 정밀도를 제공한다.
3. 직접 코딩은 주의, 소유감, 아키텍처 이해를 만든다.
   - Turnbull은 차이(diff)와 패치(patch)를 수동적으로 검토하는 방식으로는 시스템과 같은 연결감을 만들기 어렵다고 말한다.
   - 코드를 직접 쓰면 취약성이 드러난다. 사람이 코드를 깨뜨리지 않고 확장하기 어렵다면 에이전트도 어려움을 겪을 수 있다.
   - 패턴을 정리하고 일관된 원칙을 문서화하면 소프트웨어 공장이 더 잘 작동할 수 있다.
   - 디버깅(debugging)은 약한 테스트 전략을 드러내고 버그 범주 전체를 제거하게 해 준다.
4. 에이전트는 나쁜 결정을 보존하고 증폭할 수 있다.
   - Turnbull은 코딩 에이전트를 컴파일러보다는 막 합류한 인턴에 가깝다고 비교한다.
   - 에이전트는 주변 아키텍처를 개선하기보다 현재 변경을 보수적으로 처리하는 경향이 있다.
   - 글은 사람이 무심코 브라우저 로컬 스토리지(browser local storage)를 쓰자고 한 결정이 포장 코드와 간접 계층을 늘린 사례를 든다.
   - 직접 탐색하고 코드를 즐겁게 삭제한 일이 모든 것을 영어로 설명하는 방식보다 더 나은 아키텍처에 도달하도록 도왔다.
5. 소프트웨어 공장에도 여전히 세부 사항의 깊은 점검이 필요하다.
   - 에이전트는 더 많은 엔지니어가 지속적 통합(CI), 측정, 평가, 가드레일을 초기에 추가하도록 밀어붙였고, Turnbull은 이를 큰 개선으로 본다.
   - 하지만 조립 라인에는 가끔 분해하고 가까이 관찰해야 하는 약점이 있다.
   - 의미 있는 개선을 만들려면 엔지니어가 알고리즘, 성능, 테스트 실패를 자세히 들여다봐야 할 수 있다.
   - 목표는 사람이 건드릴 수 있는 것에 엄격한 경계를 긋는 것이 아니라, 미세한 구현 세부 사항을 큰 그림과 연결하는 것이다.

### 5. A Framework for Frontier AI and the Dawning of a New Age _(X Article)_

   - 이 체계는 원산지나 개방형·폐쇄형 여부와 관계없이 프런티어급 모델에 적용되고, 스타트업과 학계의 비프런티어 모델은 면제된다.
   - 글은 기술적 과제만 문제가 아니며, 희소성 이후의 삶을 둘러싼 경제적·철학적 질문도 중요하다고 말한다.
   - Hassabis는 AGI가 도착하기 전의 시간을 활용해 이 기술을 인류 전체의 이익으로 향하도록 만들어야 한다고 주장한다.
