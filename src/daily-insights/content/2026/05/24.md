## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. Trump Admin Takes Equity Stake in IBM and Other Quantum Computing Companies

1. The Commerce Department is using equity-linked grants to push quantum computing.
   - The article says the department announced more than $2 billion of investment across nine quantum-computing companies.
   - In exchange, the U.S. government receives minority, non-controlling equity stakes in each company.
   - The structure turns industrial policy into a direct ownership model rather than only a grant or procurement program.
   - Gizmodo places the quantum deal after similar equity moves involving chipmakers and rare-earth companies.
2. The policy rationale is national security and domestic supply-chain control.
   - Quantum computers are described as emerging systems that operate at the subatomic level and could eventually disrupt many industries.
   - The article notes possible breakthroughs in medicine, materials, biopharmaceutical discovery, financial modeling, and energy systems.
   - The Commerce Department also cites national-defense implications as a reason to build a stronger domestic quantum ecosystem.
   - The practical goal is not only research progress, but U.S.-based manufacturing capacity for quantum chips.
3. IBM and GlobalFoundries receive the two largest named allocations.
   - IBM receives about half of the total package, or $1 billion, to build an American quantum chip foundry.
   - IBM plans to create a new subsidiary called Anderon, headquartered in Albany, New York, to lead the foundry effort.
   - GlobalFoundries receives $375 million to establish a separate domestic quantum chip foundry.
   - These allocations show that the administration is prioritizing production infrastructure, not only early-stage research labs.
4. Several startups also receive funding for technical and manufacturing bottlenecks.
   - Atom Computing, Diraq, D-Wave, PsiQuantum, Quantinuum, and Rigetti are listed among the additional recipients.
   - The article says the funding is meant to address technical and manufacturing barriers on the path toward more capable quantum systems.
   - PsiQuantum is politically notable because Donald Trump Jr.'s venture firm 1789 Capital is an investor.
   - Senators Elizabeth Warren, Richard Blumenthal, and Andy Kim have scrutinized that investor relationship in a related defense-contract context.
5. The deal extends a broader interventionist pattern in critical industries.
   - Gizmodo says corporate intervention has become a favored Trump administration strategy for industries linked to national security and competition with China.
   - The article points to the administration becoming Intel's largest shareholder with a 10% stake in August of the previous year.
   - It also mentions rare-earth investments announced by Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick.
   - The wider implication is that strategic technology firms may increasingly face public capital tied to government ownership and political scrutiny.

### 2. State of AI 2026

1. The survey presents AI as a standard part of web-development work, with clear sampling caveats.
   - Devographics says the 2026 State of Web Dev AI survey ran from April 8 to May 8 and collected 7,258 responses.
   - The report states that the survey was open online and that respondents were not filtered or selected.
   - It also warns that an AI-focused survey is likely to attract developers who already use or care about AI.
   - The findings should therefore be read as a snapshot of a specific developer subset, not as a full measurement of the entire industry.
2. AI-assisted coding moved from occasional use toward bulk code production.
   - The report says the average share of AI-generated code rose from 28% in 2025 to 56% in 2026.
   - It says the highest-growth segments are respondents who report that 75% or more of their code is AI-generated.
   - A separate usage chart shows 6,420 respondents answering the AI-code-generation question.
   - The report also says higher AI-generation brackets correlate with lower median years of programming experience.
3. Coding agents and model competition are reshaping developer tool choices.
   - Devographics identifies Claude Code and similar coding agents as a major driver of adoption.
   - The report says Claude Code leads coding-agent sentiment, while agents may be replacing chatbots, app generators, and specialized tools for some workflows.
   - ChatGPT remains the most-used model family, but Claude has the strongest positive sentiment and leads paid model usage.
   - The models section also highlights Gemini's year-over-year progress and notes that respondents use an average of four different models.
4. AI monetization is becoming more visible to individual developers.
   - The report says personal AI spending rose compared with the previous year.
   - It specifically notes that the share of respondents spending nothing has shrunk, while the $100-$500 monthly spend group has grown.
   - Devographics connects this cost shift to coding agents, which can be more expensive than simple chatbot subscriptions.
   - The report treats rising willingness to pay as evidence of stronger product value, while also linking it to concerns about inflated AI valuations.
5. The risk section emphasizes labor, reliability, environmental, military, and bubble concerns.
   - Devographics says job displacement is a leading concern, partly because a manager's belief that AI can replace a worker may matter even if AI cannot fully do the job.
   - The report identifies hallucinations as the top practical pain point when using AI tools.
   - It also flags military use, diminished accountability, resource consumption, and environmental impact as broader risks, with women and non-men respondents more likely than men to worry about environmental impact at 58% versus 37%.
   - The report says many respondents perceive an AI bubble, but frames AI as difficult to ignore regardless of whether developers embrace it, resist it, or take a mixed position.
   - The overall message is that developers need better judgment about when AI helps, when it misleads, and how tool economics are changing.
