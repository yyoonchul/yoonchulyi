## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. Small Model Forensics

1. The experiment treats inference latency as a measurable provider behavior, not a single model label.
   - The author and Claude made roughly 2,000 timed API calls across nine small closed-weight models.
   - The test range covered prompt sizes from about 100 tokens to 1M tokens.
   - The providers included OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google-family models, with raw measurements and code linked separately.
2. Prefill scaling looks much flatter than a simple dense-attention expectation.
   - The post compares first-token latency floors against context size on log-log plots.
   - A textbook `O(n^2)` prefill expectation would bend much more sharply at large context sizes.
   - The measured curves remain sub-linear even beyond 100k-token contexts where provider overhead is less dominant.
3. Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite is the standout long-context result.
   - The article says Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite moves from 204 input tokens to 866k input tokens with latency rising only from 0.7s to 5s.
   - That is roughly a 4,200x context increase for about a 7x latency increase.
   - By comparison, GPT-4.1 Nano exceeds 23s at roughly half that context in the reported measurements.
4. Decode behavior differs by provider and context regime.
   - The post says decode cost should theoretically stay near flat or rise only slightly as prefix length grows.
   - The measurements instead show some models rising sharply and others falling at large contexts.
   - GPT-5.4 Mini reaches 108.4 ms per output token at 1 MB, while Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite is reported around 3.3 ms per token at 601 KB and 1 MB.
5. The practical takeaway is that context size, routing, and tokenizers change the cost model.
   - GPT-4.1 Nano is fastest for tiny sub-second queries, but Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite wins for large queries above about 600 KB.
   - The author infers a Google routing transition near the 100k-token band because larger Gemini Lite prompts can become faster.
   - Token counts are not provider-neutral: moving from OpenAI to Anthropic is reported to add about 14% more tokens in the author's measurements.

### 2. How do you tell who's thinking?

1. The essay starts from a management problem created by AI-assisted output.
   - Hackett argues that at least one engineer on a team may now ship work they could not reproduce on a whiteboard.
   - The code can compile, tests can pass, and the pull request can look pristine while the reasoning is not actually internalized.
   - A staff engineer's cache-design question becomes the test of whether the author has a real mental model.
2. Friction is framed as a useful signal, not only a speed limit.
   - The author says hard engineering work used to force engineers to build the model in their heads.
   - AI can remove the difficult stretch where someone wrestles with alternatives, failure modes, and second-order effects.
   - The resulting artifact can feel like understanding because it is fluent, structured, and easy to accept.
3. The essay says organizational evaluation relied on an artifact-cognition link that AI weakens.
   - Pull requests, design documents, postmortems, and incident reviews were used as samples of cognition.
   - AI output now mixes the engineer, the model, and the prompt into one artifact.
   - This matters most for juniors, because senior engineers may already have the judgment while juniors may skip the practice that builds it.
4. Hackett connects the issue to productivity studies and cognitive biases.
   - He cites a 2025 METR study where experienced developers using AI were 19% slower while believing they were 20% faster.
   - He contrasts that with McKinsey findings where less-experienced developers in unfamiliar code became 26-39% faster.
   - The psychological mechanisms include fluency illusion, lost effort signals, and authorship drift.
5. The proposed response is deeper questioning rather than AI rejection.
   - Engineers should reproduce designs cold, test uncomfortable assumptions, and ask what changes at 10x load.
   - Managers should probe whether engineers can defend non-trivial decisions live and without the document open.
   - Hiring should assume AI use and focus on whether candidates can hold the wider system in their heads.

### 3. What happens when AI starts building itself?

1. TechCrunch frames Recursive Superintelligence as a new research-heavy AI startup.
   - Richard Socher is known for founding You.com and earlier ImageNet-related work.
   - Recursive Superintelligence came out of stealth with $650 million in funding.
   - The team includes prominent AI researchers such as Peter Norvig and Cresta co-founder Tim Shi.
2. The company's stated target is recursive self-improvement rather than one-off AI assistance.
   - Socher distinguishes normal auto-research from a system that improves itself recursively.
   - The goal is to automate the full loop of research ideation, implementation, and validation.
   - The first focus is AI research itself, with eventual expansion toward broader scientific and physical-domain research.
3. Open-endedness is the technical theme behind the approach.
   - Socher cites Tim Rocktaschel's work at Google DeepMind on open-endedness and self-improvement.
   - Genie 3 is used as an example of a model that can generate interactive worlds from broad concepts.
   - The biological analogy is evolution, where adaptation and counter-adaptation produce continuing novelty.
4. Safety and evaluation are described through co-evolving AI systems.
   - Socher discusses rainbow teaming as an extension of red teaming in LLM safety.
   - One AI can try many attack angles against another AI over millions of iterations.
   - The defender can then be inoculated against broader classes of failures or unsafe outputs.
5. The business plan is not presented as a pure lab exercise.
   - Socher says he struggles with the "neolab" category and wants a viable company with useful products.
   - He says products could arrive in quarters rather than years.
   - He also argues that future recursive systems make compute allocation a central question: which problems humanity chooses to spend compute on first.

### 4. Anthropic beats OpenAI on business adoption

1. Ramp AI Index reports a clear month-over-month shift in business adoption.
   - The index uses business spend data from Ramp, including corporate card and invoice-based payments.
   - Anthropic adoption rose 3.8% in April to 34.4% of businesses.
   - OpenAI adoption fell 2.9% to 32.3%, while overall AI adoption rose 0.2 percentage points to 50.6%.
2. The article treats the result as a reversal, not a permanent crown.
   - Kharazian says Anthropic has quadrupled business adoption over the last year.
   - OpenAI business adoption grew only 0.3% over the same period in the Ramp data.
   - He warns that the AI software market is unusually dynamic and can change within months.
3. The first headwind is incentive alignment around token consumption.
   - Anthropic makes more money when customers buy more tokens.
   - That can push businesses toward more expensive models even when cheaper, faster models would be sufficient.
   - Kharazian says this risk also exists for OpenAI, but cost-cutting could hurt Anthropic's share if companies route work to cheaper options.
4. The second and third headwinds are quality, rate limits, and cost pressure.
   - The article says users recently complained about outages, rate limits, and weaker Claude results.
   - Anthropic responded by resetting usage limits in April, and a SpaceX deal is described as relieving compute constraints for the near term.
   - Kharazian also cites Rafael Hajjar's finding that Anthropic's model update could triple token costs for prompts that include images.
5. The competitive pressure may come from both OpenAI and cheaper model platforms.
   - Ramp data showed fast growth among AI inference platforms that provide access to cheap open-source models.
   - Kharazian notes that OpenAI Codex can perform similar tasks more cheaply in some developer workflows.
   - He plans to watch OpenAI market share, Codex-driven subscription growth, and cheap inference-platform growth in the next update.

3. 접근법의 기술적 주제는 개방형 진화다.
   - Socher는 Google DeepMind에서 개방형성과 자기 개선을 연구한 Tim Rocktaschel의 작업을 언급한다.
   - Genie 3는 넓은 개념에서 상호작용 가능한 세계를 생성하는 모델 사례로 제시된다.
   - 생물학적 비유는 진화이며, 적응과 역적응이 계속되는 새로움을 만든다는 설명이다.
4. 안전성과 평가는 공진화하는 AI 시스템으로 설명된다.
   - Socher는 LLM 안전성의 레드팀을 확장한 rainbow teaming을 말한다.
   - 한 AI가 다른 AI를 상대로 수백만 번의 반복 속에서 다양한 공격 각도를 만들 수 있다.
   - 방어 모델은 더 넓은 실패 유형이나 위험한 출력에 대해 면역화될 수 있다는 논리다.
5. 사업 계획은 순수 연구소로만 제시되지 않는다.
   - Socher는 "neolab" 범주에 거리감을 보이며 유용한 제품을 만드는 실행 가능한 회사를 원한다고 말한다.
   - 제품은 수년이 아니라 분기 단위로 나올 수 있다고 설명한다.
   - 그는 재귀 시스템이 발전하면 어떤 문제에 인류가 먼저 컴퓨트를 쓸지 정하는 일이 핵심 질문이 된다고 본다.

### 4. Anthropic beats OpenAI on business adoption

1. Ramp AI Index는 기업 도입률에서 뚜렷한 월간 변화를 보고한다.
   - 이 지수는 Ramp의 기업 지출 데이터를 사용하며, 법인카드와 청구서 기반 결제를 포함한다.
   - Anthropic 도입률은 4월에 3.8% 상승해 기업의 34.4%에 도달했다.
   - OpenAI 도입률은 2.9% 하락해 32.3%가 됐고, 전체 AI 도입률은 0.2%포인트 올라 50.6%가 됐다.
2. 글은 이 결과를 영구적 왕관이 아니라 반전으로 해석한다.
   - Kharazian은 Anthropic의 기업 도입률이 지난 1년 동안 4배가 됐다고 말한다.
   - 같은 기간 Ramp 데이터에서 OpenAI의 기업 도입률 성장은 0.3%에 그쳤다.
   - 그는 AI 소프트웨어 시장이 예외적으로 역동적이며 몇 달 안에도 바뀔 수 있다고 경고한다.
3. 첫 번째 역풍은 토큰 소비를 둘러싼 인센티브 정렬 문제다.
   - Anthropic은 고객이 더 많은 토큰을 살수록 더 많은 돈을 번다.
   - 이는 더 싸고 빠른 모델로 충분한 작업에서도 기업을 비싼 모델로 유도할 수 있다.
   - Kharazian은 이 위험이 OpenAI에도 있지만, 기업이 더 싼 옵션으로 라우팅하면 Anthropic 점유율이 타격을 받을 수 있다고 본다.
4. 두 번째와 세 번째 역풍은 품질, 사용량 제한, 비용 압박이다.
   - 글은 최근 사용자들이 Claude의 장애, 사용량 제한, 결과 품질 저하에 불만을 냈다고 설명한다.
   - Anthropic은 4월에 모든 사용자의 사용량 제한을 재설정했고, SpaceX와의 새 계약은 단기 컴퓨트 제약을 완화할 것으로 묘사된다.
   - Kharazian은 Rafael Hajjar의 분석을 인용해 Anthropic의 최신 모델 업데이트가 이미지 포함 프롬프트의 토큰 비용을 3배로 만들 수 있다고 말한다.
5. 경쟁 압력은 OpenAI와 저가 모델 플랫폼 양쪽에서 올 수 있다.
   - Ramp 데이터에서는 저가 오픈소스 모델 접근을 제공하는 AI 추론 플랫폼들이 빠르게 성장했다.
   - Kharazian은 OpenAI Codex가 일부 개발자 워크플로에서 같은 작업을 더 싸게 수행할 수 있다고 언급한다.
   - 그는 다음 업데이트에서 OpenAI 점유율, Codex 기반 구독 증가, 저가 추론 플랫폼 성장을 주시하겠다고 말한다.
