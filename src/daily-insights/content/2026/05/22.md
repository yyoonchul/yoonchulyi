## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. Your job Is Changing Faster Than You Think

1. Verna’s core claim is that tech jobs are being rewritten faster than titles change.
   - The essay says many people in tech may not have the same job by the end of 2027.
   - Some changes may come from layoffs, but Verna emphasizes that role content can change even when employment continues.
   - The responsibilities, tools, expectations, quality bar, and required team size are all described as moving targets.
2. Verna uses personal experience to make the change concrete.
   - Verna says parts of Verna’s own role became less relevant, moved elsewhere, were automated, or required a new operating model.
   - The result was not necessarily leaving the company, but losing older responsibilities and moving into a different version of the job.
   - The essay frames this as a warning that continuity of title can hide a deeper change in work.
3. The first response is honest reassessment of job fit.
   - Verna recommends asking whether the current job is still work the reader actually likes.
   - If roles become more competitive, Verna argues that people who love the work may outperform people merely holding on.
   - The change can also become an opening to move into a niche, switch industries, or return to individual contributor work.
4. The second response is building optionality before the job market forces it.
   - Verna says people who think full-time employment is the safe path should reconsider that assumption.
   - The essay encourages exploring independent work, solopreneurship, or some external path that creates career options.
   - The goal is not necessarily immediate departure, but reducing dependence on one employer or one narrow job definition.
5. The third response is developing AI-native clarity and execution.
   - Verna says the most important skill is creating clarity in the chaos around AI.
   - The essay warns that if a person does not know what matters or what good work looks like, AI can help execute the wrong thing faster.
   - The practical takeaway is to prepare now: ask hard questions, widen career options, and build skills that work in AI-shaped organizations.

### 2. Navigating AI with paper maps

1. Adam frames current LLM advice as unusually perishable.
   - The article says techniques that worked for one model release may become stale after the next release.
   - Adam notes that even vendor training can lag behind current model behavior.
   - The deeper criticism is that the industry spends too much energy optimizing last year’s bottlenecks.
2. The alternative is decomposition and AI solution architecture.
   - Adam uses a meal-planning example to show that one large prompt can hide several distinct tasks.
   - Parsing messy user text, solving constraints, and writing a friendly final answer do not require the same mechanism.
   - The recommended architecture is to use the right tool for each part instead of asking one prompt to do everything.
3. Prompt engineering is useful, but fragile in production.
   - Adam describes prompt work as iteration against a specific model version hosted by someone else.
   - When the model changes, the same prompt may need to be rebuilt and revalidated.
   - The article allows that prompts can be appropriate for prototypes and internal tools, while reliability and cost matter more in production.
4. RAG and workflow libraries are being reframed rather than erased.
   - Adam says earlier RAG patterns made sense when context windows were small and models forgot relevant facts.
   - Larger context windows and caching weaken the assumption that all retrieval must be guessed and packed upfront.
   - A newer pattern is to expose search, database queries, or file reads as tools behind caching so the model can request needed context.
5. The durable work is guardrails, evaluation, and integration.
   - Adam argues that capable models can often build or adjust workflows themselves, so engineering attention shifts to boundaries.
   - Important boundaries include tool schemas, permissions, validation, audit trails, halting conditions, and escalation paths.
   - The recommended learning agenda includes APIs, streaming, batching, caching, error modes, MCP, hooks, evaluation, retries, idempotency, and observability.

   - 글은 한 model release에서 통하던 technique이 다음 release 뒤에는 stale해질 수 있다고 말한다.
   - Adam은 vendor training조차 현재 model behavior보다 뒤처질 수 있다고 지적한다.

2. 대안은 decomposition과 AI solution architecture다.

   - 권장 architecture는 한 prompt에 모든 것을 맡기기보다 각 부분에 맞는 도구를 쓰는 것이다.
3. Prompt engineering은 유용하지만 production에서는 fragile하다.
   - Adam은 prompt work를 타인이 운영하는 특정 model version에 맞춰 반복 조정하는 일로 설명한다.

   - 글은 prototype과 internal tool에서는 prompt가 적절할 수 있지만, production에서는 reliability와 cost가 더 중요하다고 본다.
4. RAG와 workflow library는 사라지는 것이 아니라 재해석되고 있다.
   - Adam은 context window가 작고 model이 관련 사실을 잘 잊던 시기의 RAG pattern은 타당했다고 말한다.
   - 더 큰 context window와 caching은 retrieval을 모두 미리 추측해 prompt에 넣어야 한다는 전제를 약화한다.
   - 새로운 pattern은 search, database query, file read를 caching 뒤의 tool로 노출해 model이 필요한 context를 요청하게 하는 것이다.
5. 지속 가능한 작업은 guardrail, evaluation, integration이다.
   - Adam은 capable model이 workflow를 직접 만들거나 조정할 수 있으므로 engineering attention이 boundary로 이동한다고 주장한다.
   - 중요한 boundary에는 tool schema, permission, validation, audit trail, halting condition, escalation path가 포함된다.
   - 추천 학습 agenda는 API, streaming, batching, caching, error mode, MCP, hook, evaluation, retry, idempotency, observability다.
