## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. The Korean Telecom Giant at the Center of Anthropic's Mythos Controversy

1. The article connects Anthropic's model shutdown to a dispute over foreign access.
   - WIRED says the Trump administration's export-control move followed concern over Anthropic granting SK Telecom access to Claude Mythos.
   - Mythos is described as Anthropic's most advanced model for finding software vulnerabilities, which made access politically sensitive.
   - The White House later ordered Anthropic to restrict Mythos and Fable 5 access to US nationals, including by excluding foreign nationals inside the United States.
2. Anthropic chose to take the models offline instead of enforcing nationality-based access.
   - The company reportedly judged nationality gating difficult to implement while preserving privacy and operational practicality.
   - Mythos and Fable 5 remained offline while Anthropic and the White House negotiated over possible restoration.
   - A person close to the administration said the combination of SK Telecom concerns and Fable 5 vulnerabilities damaged trust in Anthropic's safeguards.
3. Project Glasswing was the access path that brought SK Telecom into the controversy.
   - Anthropic had restricted early Mythos access to trusted organizations through Project Glasswing.
   - SK Telecom joined roughly 150 companies in that expanded program after Anthropic worked with outside experts and the US government.
   - Other Korean participants included Samsung Electronics and the Korea Internet and Security Agency.
4. The article separates alleged national-security concern from the available public record.
   - Anthropic declined to comment, and the White House and SK Telecom did not immediately respond to WIRED's request for comment.
   - SK Telecom told a Korean newspaper that foreign-media remarks by an anonymous insider lacked verified facts and that the company has no China ties.
   - A person close to Anthropic said the US government letter demanding access restrictions did not reference SK Telecom or China.
5. SK Telecom's China history is limited in current operations but not absent.
   - WIRED says SK Telecom reported about $1.9 million in 2024 China revenue and seven China-based employees, mostly tied to investment activity.
   - The company formed UNISK with China Unicom in 2004 and invested $1 billion in China Unicom convertible bonds in 2006.
   - SK Telecom sold its China Unicom stake back in 2009, but a 2025 SEC filing still listed a roughly $17 million UNISK investment.
