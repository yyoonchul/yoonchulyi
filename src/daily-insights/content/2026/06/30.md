## 📝 Detailed Notes

### 1. The best response to AI slop, infinite advice, and online noise is from Robin Williams

1. Acunzo opens with _Good Will Hunting_ as a metaphor for AI-era content.
   - The essay centers on Sean's park-bench speech to Will, where book knowledge is contrasted with direct experience.
   - Acunzo reads the scene as a distinction between expertise and wisdom, theory and experience, knowing and living.
   - Will is described as brilliant but untested, while Sean carries the weight of events Sean has actually lived through.
2. The argument is that AI can know language without living a life.
   - Acunzo writes that AI has read the internet but cannot read a room, feel, or experience.
   - The danger is not only bad AI output; it is the cultural pressure to treat lived experience as less valuable than searchable knowledge.
   - The essay pushes back on tools and advice sellers that imply creators should distrust their own judgment.
3. Robin Williams's performance becomes the proof point.
   - Acunzo argues that another actor could have received the same script but would not have performed it the same way.
   - The words matter, but the meaning comes from choices shaped by the performer's life, memory, pain, timing, and embodiment.
   - This becomes Acunzo's broader claim about art: it is not only arranging information but turning internal experience into meaning others can access.
4. The essay separates art from generic information production.
   - Scientific discovery is framed as a search for external truth that someone else might eventually reproduce.
   - Art is framed as the synthesis of truth into meaning, where no two people bring the same life to the same material.
   - Acunzo extends this beyond traditional artists to bloggers, podcasters, business writers, and anyone trying to make work that resonates.
5. The practical challenge is to use personal experience more deliberately.
   - Acunzo says much online work feels like a script the creator forgot to act.
   - The memorable phrase is that AI has large language models while people have "little life moments."
   - The essay ends by urging creators to talk about who they are, because that is what an audience cannot get from a book, post, video, or AI snippet.

### 2. Five Archetypes for Future Product Teams

1. Cherny starts from the collapse of old functional boundaries.
   - The post says engineering, product, design, data science, and related functions are melting into a new kind of role.
   - Cherny uses the Claude Code team as an example of how product work may be organized by contribution pattern rather than job title.
   - The framing is speculative, but it is grounded in observed team roles at Anthropic.
2. The first two archetypes move ideas into reality.
   - The Prototyper creates many new ideas, most of which may not ship.
   - The Builder takes a prototype or idea and turns it quickly into production-grade product or infrastructure.
   - Together, these roles cover the early path from exploration to a shippable system.
3. The middle roles improve quality and product-market fit.
   - The Sweeper cleans up UI, simplifies code and systems, removes things, and optimizes performance.
   - The Grower iterates on an already-built product to improve product-market fit.
   - These roles matter once an idea has enough shape to need simplification, learning loops, and sharper customer fit.
4. The Maintainer owns maturity and scale.
   - The Maintainer makes mature systems secure, reliable, fast, and efficient as they grow.
   - Cherny notes that many people span two roles, and some span three.
   - The archetypes are not tied to formal function: designers, engineers, PMs, and data scientists can fall into different categories.
5. Product stage determines the healthy team mix.
   - New pre-PMF products need strength in Prototyping, Building, and Sweeping.
   - Growing products with PMF need Builders, Sweepers, Growers, and some Maintainers.
   - Products with strong PMF need Sweepers, Growers, Maintainers, and some Builders, suggesting future roles may be less domain-specific.

### 3. I think I make some of the best landing pages in startups.

1. Jeong treats the hero section as the highest-leverage part of the page.
   - The article says visitors must understand enough in the first few seconds to keep scrolling.
   - A strong hero should explain the product, create trust, and make people curious.
   - Early startups can borrow authority through YC, investors, customer logos, testimonials, or other credible signals.
2. The page should communicate transformation, not merely display UI.
   - Jeong argues that showing the whole product inside the hero often fails because visitors do not yet know what they are seeing.
   - The stronger pattern is to show the before-and-after change the product creates.
   - Jeong cites Anarlog and Granola as examples where the transformation mattered more than showing every interface detail.
3. Good landing-page design is reduction plus attention control.
   - Every button, icon, pattern, and animation competes for attention.
   - Jeong removed decorative background elements when they added more noise than value.
   - Animations should guide the eye toward what matters; if movement exists only to look cool, it probably should not move.
4. The page should tell one clear story.
   - Char's earlier page tried to explain meetings, notes, tasks, delegation, and iteration, which matched the product but overloaded the narrative.
   - The revised story is simpler: users write something down, Char understands it, and Char helps get it done.
   - Jeong argues that if visitors clearly understand one thing, they will infer broader capability, while explaining everything can make them understand nothing.
5. Positioning, sequencing, and objection handling are part of the product.
   - Char uses familiar mental models such as "AI Chief of Staff" and "Apple Notes on steroids" to make the product easier to grasp.
   - The page introduces Charlie, the AI agent inside Char, only after visitors understand the product and workflow.
   - Privacy objections are handled in plain language, the final CTA appears after visitors have invested attention, and the page keeps changing because no landing page is finished.

### 4. 2. 전문성 밖으로 나아가기

1. Toss frames technical writing as product ownership.
   - Han Juyeon leads the Knowledge System Team with developers, designers, and technical writers.
   - The role includes setting roadmap priorities, interviewing users, planning features, and building with AI tools.
   - The argument is that technical writers are especially qualified because they understand why documents fail, what good documents require, and how AI can use knowledge.
2. `todoc` was built to solve internal knowledge fragmentation.
   - Toss launched the internal document platform in October 2025 after concluding that existing tools could not scale the desired documentation culture.
   - After about six months, the platform had more than 500 documents, over 40,000 valid pages, and more than 1,000 monthly users.
   - The need came from both teams that already documented work and teams that had not yet organized their knowledge well.
3. Existing documentation systems had three core problems.
   - Static-site-generated docs required cloning repositories, editing Markdown, opening pull requests, and waiting for review, which was too high a barrier for non-engineers.
   - Existing document tools accumulated stale policies, unfinished notes, and context-free material that became documentation debt.
   - Knowledge was scattered across SSG docs, document tools, code, internal messenger discussions, and individual memory.
4. `todoc` sets four product values around knowledge usability.
   - Anyone should be able to write and edit documents easily, regardless of whether the source starts in GitHub, another document tool, or an internal messenger.
   - Knowledge in `todoc` should be easy for AI to use through team bots, API, CLI, and MCP.
   - The platform should act as an SSoT for valid internal knowledge and give each team or affiliate an extensible place to document work.
5. The remaining challenge is systematizing writing quality and freshness.
   - Toss has used technical writers' judgment to provide AI correction and draft automation, but the article says that still assumes people decide to write.
   - The next goal is for decisions, code changes, and discussions to become documents naturally inside workflows.
   - The long-term direction is to turn technical writers' expertise into systems that detect stale knowledge, suggest improvements, and keep governance healthy.

2. `todoc`은 내부 지식 fragmentation을 해결하기 위해 만들어졌다.
   - Toss는 기존 tool만으로 원하는 documentation culture를 scale하기 어렵다고 보고 2025년 10월 내부 문서 platform을 출시했다.
   - 약 반년 뒤 이 platform에는 500개 이상의 문서, 40,000개 이상의 유효 page, 월 1,000명 이상의 사용자가 있었다.
   - 필요성은 이미 문서화를 하던 team과 아직 지식을 잘 정리하지 못한 team 모두에서 나왔다.
3. 기존 documentation system에는 세 가지 핵심 문제가 있었다.
   - Static-site-generated docs는 repository clone, Markdown edit, pull request, review 대기 과정이 필요해 non-engineer에게 장벽이 높았다.
   - 기존 document tool에는 낡은 policy, 완성되지 않은 note, 맥락 없는 material이 쌓여 documentation debt가 됐다.
   - 지식은 SSG docs, document tool, code, internal messenger discussion, 개인의 기억 속에 흩어져 있었다.
4. `todoc`은 knowledge usability을 중심으로 네 가지 product value를 세운다.
   - 출발점이 GitHub, 다른 document tool, internal messenger 어디든 누구나 쉽게 문서를 쓰고 고칠 수 있어야 한다.
   - `todoc` 안의 지식은 team bot, API, CLI, MCP를 통해 AI가 쉽게 활용할 수 있어야 한다.
   - Platform은 유효한 internal knowledge의 SSoT 역할을 하고, 각 team이나 계열사가 work를 문서화할 수 있는 확장 가능한 공간을 제공해야 한다.
5. 남은 과제는 writing quality와 freshness를 system화하는 것이다.
   - Toss는 technical writer의 판단을 활용해 AI correction과 draft automation을 제공했지만, 글은 여전히 사람이 문서를 쓰기로 결심한다는 전제가 있다고 말한다.
   - 다음 목표는 decision, code change, discussion이 workflow 안에서 자연스럽게 document가 되게 만드는 것이다.
   - 장기 방향은 technical writer의 전문성을 stale knowledge 감지, improvement 제안, healthy governance 유지 system으로 바꾸는 것이다.
