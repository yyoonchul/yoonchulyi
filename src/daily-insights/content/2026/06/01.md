## рҹ“қ Detailed Notes

### 1. SkillOpt: Executive Strategy for Self-Evolving Agent Skills

1. SkillOpt reframes a skill file as the trainable object.
   - The paper argues that frontier agents often need procedural adaptation, not only better model weights or longer prompts.
   - A skill is treated as a portable natural-language artifact containing tool policies, evidence routines, formatting rules, domain heuristics, and failure modes.
   - The target model and execution harness remain frozen while a separate optimizer model edits the skill document offline.
   - The deployed result is a `best_skill.md` file, so deployment adds no optimizer calls and no weight update.
2. The optimization loop mirrors a controlled learning algorithm in text space.
   - The target agent runs rollout batches with the current skill and records trajectories, tool calls, verifier feedback, and scores.
   - The optimizer model reflects over failure and success minibatches, then proposes add, delete, or replace edits.
   - A textual learning-rate budget limits how far each update can move the skill, avoiding destructive broad rewrites.
   - A held-out selection gate accepts an edit only when validation score strictly improves, while rejected edits become negative feedback.
3. The experimental matrix is broad for a no-weight-update adaptation method.
   - The evaluation covers SearchQA, SpreadsheetBench, OfficeQA, DocVQA, LiveMathematicianBench, and ALFWorld.
   - Target models include frontier-scale GPT variants and smaller Qwen models, with direct chat, Codex, and Claude Code harnesses.
   - SkillOpt wins or ties the best measured method on all 52 model-benchmark-harness cells.
   - On GPT-5.5 direct chat, no-skill average rises from 58.8 to 82.3, a +23.5 point absolute gain.
4. The biggest gains appear on procedural tasks.
   - GPT-5.5 SpreadsheetBench rises from 41.8 to 80.7, and OfficeQA rises from 33.1 to 72.1.
   - LiveMathematicianBench rises from 37.6 to 66.9, showing that math-answer discipline can be encoded as reusable procedure.
   - In tool-backed harnesses, the paper reports +24.8 points over no skill in Codex and +19.1 in Claude Code for GPT-5.5.
   - The comparison suggests that feedback-driven skill editing matters more than simply writing a longer or stronger one-shot prompt.
5. Ablations and transfer tests support the core design.
   - Removing slow/meta update drops SpreadsheetBench sharply in the reported ablation, from 77.5 to 55.0.
   - The rejected-edit buffer gives smaller but consistent improvements, including +4.6 points on SpreadsheetBench in the default component row.
   - Optimized skills transfer across model scale, across Codex and Claude Code, and from OlympiadBench to Omni-MATH with positive gains.
   - Final skills remain small, between 379 and 1,995 tokens, and require only 1-4 accepted bounded edits in the representative runs.

### 2. AutoScientists: Self-Organizing Agent Teams for Long-Running Scientific Experimentation

1. AutoScientists targets the coordination problem in long-running scientific search.
   - The paper argues that existing agents often follow one research trajectory or depend on a central planner with fixed objectives.
   - Long-running experiments need competing hypotheses, explicit memory of failed directions, and the ability to redirect effort as evidence changes.
   - AutoScientists removes the central orchestrator agent and instead lets agents interpret a shared experimental state.
   - Teams form around promising hypotheses and can merge, split, retire, or rebalance after stagnation.
2. The system uses discussion, shared state, and role specialization.
   - Agents begin without predefined teams, read the task, current champion, and forum posts, then debate possible directions.
   - Analyst agents maintain search knowledge, audit untested axes, rank proposals by observed effect size, and queue new experiments.
   - Experiment agents claim queued proposals, modify code, run experiments, and write outcomes back to the shared log and forum.
   - Shared state includes the champion program, experiment log, research forum, team queues, dead-end registries, and hypothesis documents.
3. BioML-Bench results show gains under matched experimental budgets.
   - The benchmark spans 24 biomedical machine-learning tasks across imaging, drug discovery, protein engineering, and single-cell omics.
   - AutoScientists reaches 74.40% mean leaderboard percentile, compared with 66.07% for Autoresearch under the same task interface, model backend, and hardware budget.
   - The strongest domain-level gain is in drug discovery, where AutoScientists reaches 64.52% versus 47.91% for Biomni.
   - Protein engineering is near saturation, with both AutoScientists and Autoresearch at 96.97%, but AutoScientists has the better mean rank.
4. The GPT and ProteinGym experiments test sustained improvement from strong baselines.
   - On nanoGPT training optimization, AutoScientists reaches validation bits-per-byte near 0.978 in 34 experiments, while Autoresearch needs 65.
   - Starting from an AutoScientists champion at 0.9777, AutoScientists accepts 7 improvements over 93 experiments and reaches 0.9730.
   - In the same from-champion setting, Autoresearch accepts 0 improvements over 100 experiments and its best attempt reaches 0.9783.
   - On ACE2-Spike ProteinGym development, AutoScientists-Kermut improves Spearman correlation from 0.747 to 0.840.
5. The ablations show that the coordination mechanisms are complementary.
   - Removing analyst agents hurts TDC-hERG most, dropping AUROC from 0.867 to 0.738.
   - Removing cross-agent feedback hurts Human Plasma-Protein Binding most, dropping Pearson correlation from 0.873 to 0.714.
   - Removing self-organization hurts GPT training optimization most, worsening best validation bits-per-byte from 0.9777 to 0.9833.
   - The paper notes a cost tradeoff: AutoScientists uses more LLM tokens than Autoresearch, though the design focuses on better experiment selection under fixed experimental-compute budgets.
